Hosted image-editing models can follow the instruction ``perform a deep plane
facelift'' --- but they edit the whole face, returning a beauty-filter rendition
that misrepresents both the patient and the operation. We benchmark six
off-the-shelf hosted editors (GPT Image~2 at two quality tiers, Nano Banana~Pro
and~2, Seedream~5.0, FLUX.2~[pro]) on deep-plane-facelift and rhinoplasty
previews over real before/after photograph pairs, under a control ladder that
requires no model access: prompt-only, a client-side masked composite built from
facial landmarks, and native masked inpainting. An automated protocol scores
each edit for the two opposing failure modes --- identity drift and off-target
change --- via ArcFace cosine and a CIELAB region-localization ratio, with real
post-operative photographs as a sanity check. Across all \NumScoredEdits{}
scored edits, masked compositing pins localization at a median of
\MedLocComposite{} for every model, where prompt-only control reaches
\MedLocPromptOnly{}; the identity floor separates the editors, from FLUX.2's
aggressive edits that break it to the Nano Banana models' gentle ones that never
approach it; the mask-accepting endpoint is unexpectedly the weakest rung; and
two-procedure chains compose in either order. The results locate the capability
gap for surgical previews in \emph{control} rather than in the models, and show
the control can live entirely on the client.
