Surgeons have shown patients simulated outcomes for decades because it works:
computer imaging improves rhinoplasty patient
satisfaction~\cite{sharp2002computerimaging}, and how closely a 3D simulation
matches the eventual outcome predicts how satisfied the patient will
be~\cite{yamamichi2025crisalix}. The same literature warns that an over-idealized
preview sets expectations no operation can meet~\cite{agarwal2007morph}. Producing
these previews today requires either manual morphing skill or dedicated 3D
systems; meanwhile, general-purpose image-editing models served over public APIs
have become strikingly good at instruction-following photo edits. It is natural to
ask whether a clinic-quality preview is now one API call away.

It is not, and the reason is instructive. Asked for a specific anatomical
change, such as a facelift's jawline and neck repositioning or a dorsal hump
reduction, hosted editors return something subtly different: a flattering
overall rendition of the face, with smoothed skin and brightened features. The
edit lands everywhere. For a surgical preview this is a double failure. It
misrepresents the person (identity drift), and it misrepresents the operation,
showing changes no scalpel would make (a beauty filter). The training-free
localization literature knows how to confine
edits~\cite{avrahami2022blended,couairon2023diffedit}, but every such method needs
the model's latents, attention, or sampling loop, and a hosted API exposes none of
them.

We ask how far client-side control can carry instead, and how to measure whether
it worked. Concretely, we benchmark six hosted editors (GPT Image~2 at two
quality tiers, Nano Banana~Pro and~2, Seedream~5.0, and FLUX.2~[pro], plus a Qwen
inpainting endpoint for the mask rung) on deep plane facelift and rhinoplasty,
using real, licensed before/after photograph pairs, under a fixed seed and a full
experimental matrix. Our contributions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A model-agnostic control ladder.} Three rungs over the same
  black-box endpoints: prompt-only, a client-side masked composite
  (landmark-derived region masks, alignment, feathered blending, pose gating),
  and native masked inpainting where supported. The composite adapts the final
  step of blended-diffusion editing to a setting where nothing inside the model
  is reachable (Sec.~\ref{sec:method}).
  \item \textbf{A dual-failure-mode evaluation protocol.} ArcFace identity paired
  with a CIELAB region-localization ratio that separately catches identity drift,
  off-target beautification, and deceptive near-copies, plus a sanity check
  against real post-operative photographs and a released surgeon rubric
  (Sec.~\ref{sec:protocol}).
  \item \textbf{Benchmark findings.} Masked compositing pins edit localization at
  a median of \MedLocComposite{} across all six editors where prompt-only control
  reaches \MedLocPromptOnly{}; the identity floor separates the models, from
  FLUX.2's aggressive edits that break it to the Nano Banana models' gentle ones
  that never approach it; the mask-accepting endpoint is unexpectedly the
  weakest rung; and two-procedure chains compose in either order
  (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}).
\end{itemize}

To our knowledge this is the first controlled benchmark of off-the-shelf hosted
image-editing APIs for identity-preserving surgical previews across multiple
procedures. The claim is deliberately scoped: bespoke surgical simulators
exist~\cite{knoedler2024rhinoplastygan,agarwal2026envisage}, and hosted editors
have been benchmarked in other domains~\cite{qian2025giebench,gptimgeval2025};
the combination of black-box APIs, client-side region confinement, and clinical
ground truth is the gap this paper fills
(Sec.~\ref{sec:related}).\footnote{Analysis code and paper source:
\url{https://github.com/suxrobGM/localize-dont-beautify}}
